                                                    How the Heck Do You Choose the Right Foundation Shade? We Have Some Advice.

                                                    Published February 23, 2026

                                                    By Neha Tandon

                                                    Neha Tandon is a writer focused on beauty, covering everything from retinol serums to scalp scrubs. She once tested 24 lipsticks in one day.

                                                    Shopping for foundation can turn even the most logical person into a mystic. Unless you’re a professional makeup artist or a repeat buyer of a particular product, you may find yourself spending hours online, zooming in on computer-generated swatches and trying to discern which model’s skin tone mirrors your own. By the time you’ve hit “confirm order,” you may be inclined to throw some salt over your shoulder, blow cinnamon across your threshold, and cross a few limbs: Matching your skin tone with a foundation is never as easy as buying mascara or a moisturizer.

                                                    It can even confound our beauty journalists. When we first assembled a panel to test the best foundations, the office was abuzz with confusion as we waded through the vast network of online shade-picker quizzes.

                                                    To understand exactly how to match a foundation with various skin tones, we tapped three makeup artists who work in the highest-stakes environments — the kind where clients like Amanda Seyfried and Gabrielle Union-Wade face red carpets and 4K cameras more often than I go to my local coffee shop. Daniel Martin, Sam Fine, and Sandhya Shekar frequently paint the faces of the perpetually photographed, so they can’t afford to make a mistake. The easiest way to test shades is by visiting a store and doing it in person, but even that can be confusing. Below, we provide guidance to help you navigate the makeup aisles  — whether they’re digital or IRL — with more confidence.

                                                    Start with skin type and finish

                                                    Finding a foundation can be like finding a life partner: Before you dive in, you must know yourself.

                                                    Every makeup artist we spoke to said that people won’t be happy with a shade if they don’t like how it sits on their skin. So start with your skin type and the finish of the foundation. Also remember that some combinations work better than others. For example, if you have dry skin, a matte-finish foundation might highlight parched, flakey spots. Someone with oily skin may find that “luminous” formulas end up being greasy before lunch hour.

                                                    Fine recommended looking for descriptors that reflect your personality. Do you want full coverage, glazed-doughnut radiance, or something in between? (If you’re stuck, take a look at our guide to the best foundations, which has options for everyone.)

                                                    Identify your true tone

                                                    Once you know which finish you’re seeking, it’s time to choose your shade family, from fair, light, medium, dark, and so on. And an in-person visit remains the most failsafe way to figure out which shade suits you the best.

                                                    Shade nomenclature is frustratingly non-standard, and descriptions like “fair,” “medium,” and “deep medium” can vary widely: One brand’s “medium” can be another brand’s “light,” and “fair” can be anything from beige to alabaster. On top of that, faces are often a mosaic of colors. You may have purple under your eyes, red across your cheeks, and dark spots lining your jaw. So where you swatch matters.

                                                    Resist the urge to test on the backs of your hands (which typically see more sun and get less skin care than your face). Instead, try to match your “true tone” by finding a patch of skin that has no hyperpigmentation or redness, Shekar said. Fine and Martin both suggested matching each shade to your jawline, due to its proximity to the rest of your body. “The last thing you want is your face to not match your body in photos,” Martin said. The jaw acts as the bridge between the face and décolleté, so matching a shade to the jawline will help ensure a consistent look overall.

                                                    Choose your undertone

                                                    After you’ve chosen a finish, you’ll often have a few options for undertones — usually marked as “cool,” “neutral,” or “warm” (and sometimes “pink” or “olive”). These are the subtle tints in a foundation shade: pink or blue in cool tones, yellow or peach in warm tones, and a mix of all of those in neutral tones.

                                                    To find out what your undertones are, traditional advice would tell you to look at your veins or to put a white piece of paper to your face. But in practice, even we beauty editors have found that advice to be lacking. The reality is that undertones are inconsistent from brand to brand, and they may even show through differently, depending on your skin concerns. To account for that, instead of trying to directly match your undertone, Martin suggested considering undertone labels (cool, neutral, warm) as correctors.

                                                    If you struggle with redness or acne scarring, you can go one of two ways: either neutral or warm tones. Both can help counteract the appearance of inflammation or pigmentation, but the amount of warmth you need will depend on your specific skin. Although they are less universally used, cool-toned foundations are best for those with naturally rosy tints who do not have significant redness to neutralize.

                                                    Swatch, swatch, swatch

                                                    Trial and error is, unfortunately, a necessary evil when it comes to foundation shopping, especially with a brand you haven’t tried before.

                                                    If you are testing in person, Fine recommended the rule of three: Apply three potential shades along the jawline (or another true-tone patch of skin), and see which one seems to disappear.

                                                    If you’re shopping online, choose retailers with flexible return policies, such as Sephora and CVS. These retailers allow you to return gently used makeup. So, depending on your budget, you can either order three shades and return two, or you can buy and return single shades.

                                                    No matter where you test, remember that lighting is key. Fine, Martin, and Shekar all warned that the lighting in stores and bathrooms can be deceptive. Before you make your final choice, be sure to look at each shade in natural lighting. If you’re in a store, you can accomplish this by walking over to a window and snapping a selfie.

                                                    Choosing a foundation shade doesn’t have to be an exercise in alchemy. Think of these tips not as a rigid science but as a stack of spells meant to work together to build that second skin. Uncross your limbs! You’ll know the mystery is gone the moment a foundation performs a disappearing act on your face.
